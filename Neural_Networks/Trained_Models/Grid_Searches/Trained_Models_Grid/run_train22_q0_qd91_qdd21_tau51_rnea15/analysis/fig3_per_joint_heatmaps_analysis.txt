The figure displays heatmaps that provide a granular, joint-by-joint analysis of the Test RMSE (a) and Train RMSE (b) across the five robot joints (J1 to J5) for the three evaluated architectures. The color scale intuitively maps performance, ranging from green (best/lowest error) to red (worst/highest error).

(a) Test RMSE: Across all architectures, Joint J2 (shoulder) consistently exhibits the highest prediction error, indicated by the dark red hues. This is likely due to the complex dynamics and larger inertial forces associated with this proximal joint. J5 and J3 follow as the next most challenging joints, while J4 (wrist) consistently shows the lowest error (dark green). Crucially, when comparing architectures, the Residual-Corr model consistently achieves lower Test RMSE across most individual joints, notably on the challenging J2 (0.149 N·m), compared to the Black-Box model (0.151 N·m). The Physics-Reg model also performs robustly, generally comparable to Residual-Corr. The Black-Box model visually exhibits the warmest colors (highest errors) across the board on the test set.

(b) Train RMSE: The Train RMSE heatmap reveals a contrasting, almost inverted pattern compared to the test set. Here, the Black-Box model achieves the lowest training errors across virtually all joints (e.g., J2: 0.118 N·m, J1: 0.052 N·m), appearing visually "greener" overall. The Residual-Corr and Physics-Reg models show slightly higher training errors, reflecting their regularized nature.

Conclusion: The per-joint heatmap analysis corroborates the aggregate metrics while offering deeper insights into the spatial distribution of errors. It confirms that the Black-Box model's lower overall training error is achieved by overfitting across all individual joints, which predictably translates to higher errors on the test set for every single joint. The physics-informed models (Residual-Corr and Physics-Reg) effectively mitigate this overfitting. By constraining the learning process, they achieve more robust, balanced, and superior generalization across the entire robotic manipulator, particularly on dynamically complex joints like the shoulder.

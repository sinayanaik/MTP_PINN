The plot displays three subplots (a), (b), and (c) illustrating the training and validation Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss across epochs for three distinct neural network architectures: Residual-Corr, Physics-Reg, and Black-Box, respectively.

(a) Residual-Corr: Both training and validation losses decrease steadily and remain remarkably close to each other throughout the training process. The "best epoch" for validation loss is achieved relatively late in training (around epoch 319). The minimal gap between the training and validation loss curves suggests excellent generalization and an absence of overfitting.

(b) Physics-Reg: The loss curves exhibit a sharper initial drop compared to the Residual-Corr model. The validation loss tracks the training loss well in the early epochs but eventually plateaus, with the optimal epoch occurring very late (around epoch 1071). While a slight gap emerges between the training and validation losses, it remains stable, indicating that the model avoids severe overfitting and maintains good generalization.

(c) Black-Box: This purely data-driven model shows the most aggressive decrease in training loss, eventually reaching the lowest training MSE among all three architectures. However, the validation loss begins to diverge and increase early in the training process, resulting in the "best epoch" occurring much earlier (around epoch 73). The substantial and growing gap between the training and validation loss curves is a classic indicator of pronounced overfitting; the model excessively memorizes the training data at the expense of its ability to generalize to unseen validation data.

Conclusion: Qualitatively and quantitatively, the physics-informed models (Residual-Corr and Physics-Reg) demonstrate vastly superior generalization capabilities compared to the purely data-driven Black-Box model. The inductive bias introduced by incorporating physical principles effectively regularizes the learning process, preventing the severe overfitting observed in the Black-Box architecture.

The figure provides supplementary error metrics through bar charts, comparing the architectures based on Mean Absolute Error (MAE) in subplot (a) and Normalized RMSE (NRMSE) in subplot (b). Solid bars denote Training performance, while hatched bars represent Test performance.

(a) MAE per Architecture (N·m): The Mean Absolute Error confirms previous observations. The purely data-driven Black-Box model achieves the lowest Train MAE (0.0443 N·m) but the highest Test MAE (0.0719 N·m). In contrast, the physics-informed models exhibit slightly higher Train MAEs (Physics-Reg: 0.0474 N·m, Residual-Corr: 0.0480 N·m) but achieve significantly lower, superior Test MAEs (Physics-Reg: 0.0673 N·m, Residual-Corr: 0.0648 N·m). The Residual-Corr architecture performs the best overall on the unseen test set.

(b) NRMSE per Architecture: The NRMSE metric, which normalizes the error to provide a scale-independent perspective, illustrates an identical trend. The Black-Box model records the lowest Train NRMSE (0.0496) but fails to generalize, yielding the highest Test NRMSE (0.0943). The Residual-Corr model, despite having the highest Train NRMSE (0.0566), achieves the lowest and best Test NRMSE (0.0825), closely followed by the Physics-Reg model (0.0855).

Conclusion: The MAE and NRMSE metrics strongly corroborate the findings derived from the standard RMSE and R² analyses. The Black-Box model consistently memorizes the training data better but suffers from poor generalization. The physics-guided architectures—particularly the Residual-Corr model—successfully trade a marginal increase in training error for a substantial, robust improvement in generalization to unseen dynamic trajectories, as evidenced by their significantly lower test MAE and NRMSE values.

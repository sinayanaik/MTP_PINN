The plot investigates the "Physics Weight Impact" within the Physics-Regularized FNN architecture. It charts the Test RMSE against varying values of the Physics Regularization Weight ($\lambda$) across different Training Data Fractions (represented by distinct colored lines).

Shape of the Curves: The most prominent feature of this visualization is the distinct "U-shape" exhibited by almost all curves. As the physics weight $\lambda$ increases from a negligible value (e.g., 0.05) towards moderate values (e.g., 0.2 to 0.5), the Test RMSE steadily decreases, indicating improved generalization. However, as $\lambda$ increases further towards 1.0 or 2.0, the error begins to rise again.

Optimal Regularization ($\lambda$): The nadir of the U-shaped curves consistently falls within the $\lambda$ range of 0.2 to 0.5, regardless of the data fraction used. This identifies a critical "sweet spot" where the loss function optimally balances the data-driven MSE loss with the physics-informed regularization penalty. Too little weight (e.g., < 0.1) fails to provide sufficient inductive bias, while too much weight (e.g., > 1.0) over-constrains the neural network, excessively forcing it to adhere to the simplified rigid-body model and preventing it from learning unmodeled residual dynamics (such as complex friction) present in the real data.

Data Fraction Impact: The U-shape is most pronounced and steep for smaller data fractions (e.g., 2%, indicated by the dark blue line). When empirical data is scarce, the model's reliance on the correct physical prior is paramount, making precise tuning of $\lambda$ critical. For larger data fractions (e.g., 100%, yellow line), the curve is slightly flatter, reflecting the network's increased ability to rely on the abundant data, though the optimal weight remains anchored around 0.5.

Conclusion: The analysis reveals that the efficacy of the Physics-Reg architecture is highly sensitive to the tuning of the regularization hyperparameter $\lambda$. Achieving robust generalization requires an optimal balance—typically between 0.2 and 0.5—that successfully integrates prior physical knowledge without overwhelmingly restricting the network's capacity to learn from empirical data.
